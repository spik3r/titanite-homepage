Cryptocurrency & Bitcoin Basics

Crypto, Blockchain, Bitcoin basics explained.

Key words explained:

A cryptocurrency (commonly known as crypto) is a form of digital asset class designed to be decentralised and work as a store of value and work as a secure way to send financial transactions.

The biggest and most well known cryptocurrency is Bitcoin. It was created as a result of the 2008 GFC by an unknown individual named Satoshi Nakamoto. Bitcoin can be traded on a variety of exchanges similar to stocks and bonds, used to purchase goods online and is often used in countries struggeling economically like Venezuela as a store of value to avoid Hyperinflation. A more technical explanation can be found here: https://bitcoin.org/bitcoin.pdf

A blockchain is a continuously growing list of blocks, which are start at block 0 also known as the Genesis block. The blocks are linked and secured through hashes pointing to the previous block. The Bitcoin Blockchain and most others are public meaning anyone can see all transactions and how much Bitcoin another address has or had.

How it works

Bitcoin or the Blockchain can be thought of like a torrent, once something is on the blockchain it is distributed to every node. Since there are almost 10'000 bitcoin nodes that all have a copy of the blockchain is is extremely unlikely for something once on the blockchain to be altered or taken down, just like it's extremely hard for record companies to remove pirated music from the internet since the files are distributed over many computers around the world.

Ethereum, Tokens and Smart contracts

A brief summary of key things related to Ethereum

Ethereum is a second generation cryptocurrency, meaning it is newer than bitcoin and aims to improve some things the developers didn't like about Bitcoin. Probably the most well know and important feature of ethereum are DAapps or decentralized applications.

A DAPP is basically an application deployed on the Ethereum Blockchain (in this case) which has all the benefits of decentralization but also some limitations which I'll discuss in the section about smart contracts. A key feature of a DAPP is they are trustless, meaning you can write a dapp to pay out money when a certain condition is met, one of the common examples for popular dapps is gambling websites. These are extemely cost effective compared to non dapps since there doesn't need to be a human check and issue the winnings the contract can do this automatically.

Tokens are Essentially cryptocurrencies running on the infrastructure of the Ethereum Blockchain. These are often used in games or as an easy way to make your own cryptocurrency on a proven secure network.

Smart Contracts are the way we write DAPPs in case of Ethereum DAPPs this will most likely be done in the Programming language Solidity. Solidity is a JavaScript-like scripting language. The guys from loom made an excellent tutorial: https://cryptozombies.io/

Trading & Technical analysis basics

A few core concepts about TA and trading explained.

A word of caution

Everything here is a guide and not pure science, these things are meant as a guide but do not always work. This is also not financial advice and intended to be for educational use only. I recommend always checking at least 2 different indicators as they sometimes consider different things one might be telling you it’s a good time to buy where another could indicate the opposite.

Indicators

Charts

For charting I use Tradingview Use candles please DON’T use the line graph :) Candles tell you so much more! For example:
  • The volume,
  • The high & low of that time frame,
  • The start and end price of that time frame
  • Outliers in the price (The price might have gone up but then reversed).
  • candlestick patterns,

Support & Resistance

Support and resistance are regions of price where the price hasn't managed to break through. For example if the price of a stock is currently $1.20 and every time it goes down near the $1.00 range it quickly bounces back up. If the price tests the $1.00 rage often enough it might eventually break through, but the more often it tests this and gets held back the stronger the support gets for future tests. This is because other traders trying to "Buy low & Sell high" will be watching the area and have buy orders waiting to pick up the stock at the place it normally turns back around to sell it later for a profit.

Trends

Try to draw a line touching the top of at least 3 candles for down trend (the bottom for up trend), the more candles the line touches the stronger the trend. This is a simple way to see if there is a change in trend (A candle breaks through the line by more than a little bit) It's usually a good idea to trade with the trend. Some of the simplest and most basic strategies are the most effective (Trend following)

Moving Averages

The moving average tells you the average price over the last x candles. The most important & widely used are the 21, 50, 100 and 200 Moving averages. These also often act as strong support or resistances.

RSI

RSI tells us if something is overbought (about to go down) or oversold(about to go). Again oversimplified it’s the distance of the current price from the moving average price. The normal price range is from 30-70 below 30 is oversold and above 70 is overbought.

IDE's, terminal and other tools

Learn to use your tools effectively.

I went through a phase of trying every new IDE & different editors thinking that would make me a better programmer. This held me back because every tool is slightly different usually has different shortcuts which you can sometimes map to the ones you're familiar with, but never works 100% the same. For this reason my choice of IDE is IntelliJ which when you spend the $100 on the Ultimate version (or your employer does) works great for almost every language & framework. There are add on's to connect to and inspect databases like mongo & postgres, start servers like Express, Flask or SpringBoot and even do http requests as a postman like client you can check in to your code base. I'm not saying you must use intelliJ, but I definitely recommend find one IDE or editor and try to use it for most of your projects and LEARN the shortcuts to common tasks you use frequently.
